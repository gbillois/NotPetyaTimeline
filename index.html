Phase 1

Prelude & Staging (2016 – June 2017)

2016 Prelude
Sandworm Plants Backdoor in MEDoc
Russia's GRU hacking unit Sandworm (Unit 74455) compromises the update infrastructure of M.E.Doc (Medoc), Ukraine's dominant accounting software used by ~80% of Ukrainian businesses for tax filing. The backdoor enables covert data exfiltration and future code execution.
Apr 14, 2017 Prelude
Shadow Brokers Leak NSA Exploits
The Shadow Brokers publish a cache of stolen NSA cyberweapons including EternalBlue — a critical exploit targeting the SMBv1 protocol (CVE-2017-0144) affecting unpatched Windows machines. Microsoft had issued patch MS17-010 in March 2017, but millions of systems remain vulnerable.
May 12, 2017 Predecessor
WannaCry Ransomware Strikes Globally
WannaCry — also leveraging EternalBlue — infects over 200,000 systems across 150 countries in a single day, including the UK's NHS. Attributed to North Korea's Lazarus Group. WannaCry serves as an inadvertent proof-of-concept for EternalBlue's destructive potential just six weeks before NotPetya.
Jun 22, 2017 Staging
Malicious MEDoc Update Prepared
Sandworm inserts NotPetya malware into a legitimate MEDoc software update (version 10.01.189). The trojanized update is signed and staged on MEDoc's official servers, scheduled for distribution to approximately 1 million computers across Ukrainian businesses and government entities.
Phase 2

Zero Hour — June 27, 2017

Jun 27 · ~10:30 AM Kyiv Outbreak
NotPetya Detonates via MEDoc Update
The trojanized MEDoc update triggers the NotPetya payload. Within minutes, computers begin displaying a fake "Check Disk" screen while the malware overwrites the Master Boot Record (MBR) and encrypts the Master File Table (MFT), rendering machines permanently unbootable. There is no real decryption key — this is a wiper, not ransomware.
Jun 27 · 10:30–11:00 AM Spread
Ukraine's Infrastructure Begins Falling
Simultaneous outages hit Ukrainian institutions within the first 30 minutes: Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant radiation monitoring goes offline; Ukrainian National Bank, Ukrenergo (power grid), Ukrtelecom, Kyiv Boryspil International Airport, and multiple Ukrainian government ministries lose systems.
Jun 27 · ~11:00 AM onward Lateral Movement
EternalBlue + Mimikatz Propagate Globally
NotPetya uses a dual-propagation strategy far more sophisticated than WannaCry: EternalBlue exploits unpatched SMBv1 to hop between machines on the network, while a modified Mimikatz credential harvester extracts Windows login credentials from memory to spread as a legitimate user even on fully-patched machines. A single infected laptop inside a corporate network can compromise the entire domain.
Jun 27 · Afternoon Global Spread
Maersk IT Infrastructure Annihilated
A.P. Møller-Maersk, the world's largest container shipping company, loses virtually its entire global IT infrastructure. 45,000 PCs, 4,000 servers, and 2,500 applications are wiped. The company handles roughly 20% of global seaborne trade — port operations freeze at 76 ports worldwide. CEO Søren Skou calls it "an unprecedented attack."
Jun 27 · Afternoon Global Spread
Merck, TNT, Mondelez, and Others Fall
Merck (pharmaceutical) loses ~30,000 computers, halting manufacturing including flu vaccine production. TNT Express (FedEx subsidiary) suffers months of disruption. Mondelez International loses 1,700 servers and 24,000 laptops. Reckitt Benckiser, Saint-Gobain, Rosneft, and Heritage Valley Health System (Pittsburgh hospitals) are also struck.
Phase 3

Emergency Response (June 27 – July 2017)

Jun 27 · Evening Response
CERT-UA and Global IR Teams Mobilize
Ukraine's Computer Emergency Response Team issues an advisory. Security firms including ESET, Cisco Talos, CrowdStrike, and Kaspersky begin real-time analysis. Emergency network segmentation and system shutdowns are ordered at affected organizations globally. A "kill switch" domain (perfc.dat) is identified — machines that find this file on the local disk will abort, offering partial containment.
Jun 27–28 Response
Maersk Begins Manual Cargo Operations
Maersk staff revert to pen and paper to track container movements. The company flies a team to Ghana — the sole Maersk office whose domain controller survived (due to a power outage during the attack) — to recover Active Directory and begin reinstalling their global IT infrastructure from scratch over the next 10 days.
Late Jun – Jul 2017 Recovery
Weeks-Long Recovery Efforts
Organizations face recovery timelines measured in weeks, not days. Merck is forced to borrow flu vaccine stock from the U.S. government's strategic national stockpile. TNT Express customer operations remain degraded for months. The NHS in the UK advises partners on containment. Heritage Valley Health postpones non-urgent medical procedures.
Phase 4

Investigation & Attribution (2017–2018)

Jul – Oct 2017 Analysis
Security Researchers Confirm "NotPetya Is a Wiper"
ESET, Cisco Talos, and Comae Technologies independently confirm that despite displaying a $300 ransom demand, NotPetya has no decryption capability. The installation ID shown on the ransom screen is randomly generated — paying is futile. Researchers also trace the MEDoc supply-chain vector and identify three separate propagation methods: MEDoc update, EternalBlue, and WMIC/PSEXEC with harvested credentials.
Jan 2018 Attribution
CIA Attributes NotPetya to GRU Sandworm
The U.S. Central Intelligence Agency concludes with high confidence that the Russian military intelligence agency GRU's Sandworm unit (also known as APT28 subgroup / Voodoo Bear) is responsible for NotPetya. Investigators tie the attack to Russia's ongoing hybrid war against Ukraine.
Feb 15, 2018 Attribution
Five Nations Formally Attribute Attack to Russia
The United States, United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, and Denmark simultaneously issue official statements attributing NotPetya to the Russian military. The White House calls it "the most destructive and costly cyber-attack in history." Russia denies all involvement.
Phase 5

Legal Aftermath (2019–2022)

2019 Insurance
Mondelez vs. Zurich — The "Act of War" Dispute
Mondelez International files suit against insurer Zurich American after Zurich denies a $100M+ cyber-insurance claim, citing a war exclusion clause — arguing that NotPetya was an "act of war" by a sovereign nation. The landmark case raises industry-wide questions about whether cyberattacks sponsored by states are insurable events. The case is settled out of court in 2022.
Oct 19, 2020 Indictment
DOJ Indicts 6 GRU Officers for NotPetya
The U.S. Department of Justice unseals an indictment charging six GRU officers (Unit 74455 / Sandworm) with crimes related to NotPetya, the 2018 Olympic Destroyer attack, the French election interference, and attacks on Ukrainian critical infrastructure. Officers named include Yuriy Andrienko, Sergei Detistov, Pavel Frolov, Anatoliy Kovalev, Artem Ochichenko, and Petr Pliskin. None are in U.S. custody.
Ongoing Lessons
Supply-Chain Security Becomes a Board-Level Issue
NotPetya fundamentally reshapes enterprise cybersecurity strategy. Organizations accelerate network segmentation, zero-trust architecture adoption, offline backup requirements, and software supply-chain auditing. CISA and NIST reference NotPetya as a case study in critical infrastructure protection. The attack remains the clearest modern example of a cyberweapon causing kinetic-scale economic damage.

Attack Anatomy

Key Techniques

Supply-Chain Compromise
MEDoc update server backdoored to distribute malware to ~1M systems as a trusted software update.
EternalBlue (CVE-2017-0144)
NSA-developed SMBv1 exploit propagates NotPetya laterally across unpatched Windows networks.
Mimikatz Credential Harvesting
Extracts plaintext credentials from Windows LSASS memory, enabling spread even on patched machines.
WMIC / PsExec Lateral Movement
Uses harvested admin credentials with Windows management tools to execute payload remotely.
MBR / MFT Overwrite
Overwrites Master Boot Record and encrypts Master File Table, permanently destroying boot capability.
Fake Ransomware Cover
Displays a Bitcoin ransom demand to disguise the true wiper nature and delay incident response.
